Being the subject of an allegation can feel distressing and overwhelming.

It is important to remember:

- Allegations must always be taken seriously.
- You are entitled to fair process and support.
- Clear procedures exist to protect children and carers.

Your role is to **report immediately, cooperate fully, and seek support appropriately**.

### ğŸš¨ What Is an Allegation?

An allegation may involve a claim that you have:

- Harmed a child.
- Behaved in a way that may have harmed a child.
- Possibly committed a criminal offence against a child.
- Behaved in a way that suggests you may pose a risk to children.

Allegations may come from:

- A child in your care.
- Another child.
- A birth family member.
- A professional.
- A third party.

All allegations must follow safeguarding procedures.

---

### âš¡ Immediate Actions

If an allegation is made:

1. **Inform your Supervising Social Worker immediately.**
2. If outside office hours, contact the Out-of-Hours service.
3. Make a factual written record of:
   - What was said
   - Who was present
   - Date and time
4. Do not discuss the matter with others (except your supervising social worker or designated support).

Do not attempt to investigate or defend yourself directly to the child.

---

### ğŸš« What Not To Do

Do not:

- Confront the child about the allegation.
- Discuss the situation with friends, other carers, or on social media.
- Contact birth family members about the issue.
- Try to influence witnesses.

Maintaining professional boundaries protects everyone involved.

### ğŸ¤ Your Rights & Support

You are entitled to:

- Be informed of the allegation (as appropriate).
- Fair and transparent procedures.
- Independent support.
- Advice from your fostering service.
- Access to legal advice (where appropriate).

You may also access:

- Independent support services for foster carers.
- Your professional association (if applicable).
- Counselling or wellbeing support.

Being subject to an allegation does not mean wrongdoing has occurred.
