Children in care may have experienced abuse or neglect before coming into foster care.  

Understanding the different types of abuse helps you recognise signs early and respond appropriately.

If you are ever unsure, **record and report your concerns immediately**.

---

### üõë Physical Abuse

Physical abuse involves deliberately causing physical harm to a child.

This may include:

- Hitting, shaking, throwing, burning or scalding
- Poisoning
- Suffocating
- Fabricating or inducing illness
- Physical punishment that causes injury

Possible signs:

- Unexplained injuries
- Injuries inconsistent with explanation
- Frequent bruising
- Fear of certain adults
- Wearing long sleeves in warm weather to hide marks

---

### üíî Emotional Abuse

Emotional abuse is the persistent emotional maltreatment of a child that affects their development.

This may include:

- Humiliation or constant criticism
- Rejection or ignoring
- Threats or intimidation
- Exposure to domestic abuse
- Serious bullying (including online)
- Exploitation or corruption

Possible signs:

- Low self-esteem
- Withdrawal or extreme behaviour
- Developmental delay
- Self-harm
- Excessive need for approval

Emotional abuse can be as harmful as physical abuse.

---

### üö´ Sexual Abuse

Sexual abuse involves forcing or enticing a child to take part in sexual activities.

This includes:

- Physical sexual contact
- Non-contact activities (e.g., showing pornography)
- Grooming (online or offline)
- Exploitation for sexual purposes
- Encouraging sexualised behaviour

Possible signs:

- Age-inappropriate sexual behaviour
- Sexual knowledge beyond developmental stage
- Sudden behavioural changes
- Fear of specific individuals
- Sexually transmitted infections

---

### ü•Ä Neglect

Neglect is the persistent failure to meet a child‚Äôs basic physical and/or psychological needs.

This may include:

- Lack of adequate food, clothing or shelter
- Failure to supervise appropriately
- Poor hygiene
- Lack of medical care
- Emotional unavailability

Possible signs:

- Poor growth
- Frequent hunger
- Poor hygiene
- Untreated medical issues
- Tiredness or falling asleep at school

Neglect is the most common form of abuse.

---

### üíª Online Abuse

Online abuse can occur through:

- Social media
- Gaming platforms
- Messaging apps
- Image sharing

Risks include:

- Grooming
- Exploitation
- Cyberbullying
- Sexting and image-based abuse
- Radicalisation

Children in care may be particularly vulnerable to online exploitation.

---

### üí∞ Criminal Exploitation (CCE)

Criminal exploitation involves children being manipulated into criminal activity.

This may include:

- County lines drug trafficking
- Theft
- Violence
- Carrying weapons

Possible signs:

- Unexplained money or gifts
- Sudden new friendships
- Going missing frequently
- Secrecy about whereabouts
- Use of drug-related language

---

### üíî Sexual Exploitation (CSE)

Child Sexual Exploitation involves coercion or manipulation into sexual activity in exchange for something.

This may involve:

- Gifts, money, alcohol or drugs
- Emotional manipulation
- Threats or violence
- Online exploitation

Children may not recognise they are being exploited.

---

### üß† Domestic Abuse Exposure

Children who witness domestic abuse experience emotional harm.

They may:

- Feel responsible
- Become anxious or withdrawn
- Display aggression
- Struggle with attachment

Domestic abuse exposure is a safeguarding concern even if the child is not physically harmed.

---

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Radicalisation

Radicalisation is when a child is influenced into extreme views that may lead to harm.

Warning signs may include:

- Extreme ideological views
- Isolation
- Secretive online activity
- Sudden change in language or behaviour

This falls under the Prevent Duty.

---

### üìù What To Do If You Suspect Abuse

If you notice signs of abuse:

1. Record your concerns factually.
2. Include dates, times, and observations.
3. Inform your Supervising Social Worker immediately.
4. If there is immediate danger, call 999.

You are not responsible for investigating ‚Äî only for recognising and reporting concerns.

---

### üõ° Why This Matters

Under the Fostering Services (England) Regulations 2011 and National Minimum Standards:

- Foster carers must safeguard children.
- Concerns must be reported promptly.
- Children must be protected from all forms of harm.

Ofsted will expect to see:

- Awareness of abuse types
- Clear recording
- Timely reporting
- Appropriate professional boundaries
