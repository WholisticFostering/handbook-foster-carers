Every child in care must have a clear plan.

This usually includes:
- A Care Plan
- A Placement Plan
- A Health Plan
- A Personal Education Plan (PEP)
- A Pathway Plan (for older young people preparing for independence)

The Care Plan sets out the long-term plan for the child.

Foster carers:
- Contribute to planning
- Attend meetings and reviews
- Help children understand their plan
- Support children to share their views

Plans should be shared with carers and reviewed regularly.

See:
- Care Planning Policy
- Placement Planning Policy

## ğŸ  The Placement Plan

The Placement Plan explains how the child will be cared for day-to-day.

It covers:
- Delegated authority (what decisions you can make)
- Health arrangements
- Education
- Contact with family
- House rules
- Financial arrangements (e.g. pocket money, clothing)

It should be agreed at the start of placement (or shortly after if urgent).

Foster carers must understand:
- What decisions they can make independently
- What requires social worker agreement

See:
- Delegated Authority Guidance
- Placement Planning Policy

Maintaining family relationships is usually important.

Contact arrangements are agreed as part of the Care Plan.

Contact may:
- Take place in the foster home
- Take place at a contact centre
- Be supervised
- Be unsupervised
- Be indirect (letters, calls, online)

Contact can be emotional for children.

Foster carers support children by:
- Preparing them beforehand
- Helping them process feelings afterwards
- Keeping routines stable

If contact becomes unsafe or distressing, inform your SSW immediately.

- Contact Policy
